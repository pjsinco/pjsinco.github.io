On a gorgeous, perfect Sunday afternoon for football at Soldier Field, an effort that was far from pretty wound up getting the job done for the Chicago Bears in a 24-17 victory over the Tennessee Titans to begin the season.

The much-hyped debut of No. 1 draft pick Caleb Williams will be remembered more for a defensive surge in the second half, a big pick-me-up from special teams and mistakes the rookie quarterback avoided as the Bears rallied from a 17-point deficit to win their opener for the first time since 2020 in Detroit.

The difference was that young Titans quarterback Will Levis, a second-round draft pick in 2023, made catastrophic mistakes in the second half. The Bears forced him into some of those miscues and then pounced, as the defense picked up an offense whose effort would not be good enough to beat many teams.

Here are 10 thoughts after the opener.

1. Most of Caleb Williams’ preseason highlights came on extended plays outside the pocket.

Williams would create time and space and then flash his gifted arm talent to make big plays.

“He’s had some wow throws, there have definitely been some of those,” an assistant general manager for another NFC team said after the preseason. “Watching it, you’re wondering if he’s going to be like almost all of these young quarterbacks. They want to see it before they throw it and he’s not going to trust it otherwise. It almost seemed what he wanted to do was go Aaron Rodgers, go off schedule and find the guy. Backyard football.”

The “wow” throws weren’t there Sunday, and I’d bet that was an emphasis of the Titans coaching staff: Contain Williams in the pocket and make him play from there.

The final statistics weren’t pretty. He completed 14 of 29 passes for 93 yards with a long completion of 13. The only positive that stood out — and it should not be brushed aside — was Williams did not commit a turnover. Kickoff returner Velus Jones Jr. was responsible for the team’s only giveaway; if the Bears had one on offense, it probably would have been a killer.

Williams remained poised despite the slow start and didn’t appear to press, which often leads to forced mistakes. The Bears won despite totaling only 148 yards of offense, which typically leads to a loss.

Since 2000, teams with less than 150 yards of offense are 20-148 (a .119 winning percentage), with the Bears having an inordinate number of games that fit in that category. They’re now 2-10 when failing to reach 150 yards in that span. The previous victory was a 23-13 win over the Minnesota Vikings on Dec. 3, 2006, a day Devin Hester scored on a punt return and Lovie Smith’s defense totaled five takeaways to back Rex Grossman, who was 6 of 19 passing for 34 yards with three interceptions.

“He’s smart as a whip and knows the offense,” coach Matt Eberflus said of Williams. “And we’ve just got to keep playing well around him as he grows and reinvests and improves. He’s going to learn a lot these first three, four games in terms of the NFL looks and speed and all the things we have to do.”

Keenan Allen dropped what looked like a touchdown on second-and-goal from the 6-yard line in the second quarter. Teams count on a veteran wide receiver to make a play in that situation, and the ball just went off his hands, leading the Bears to settle for a field goal after a 67-yard kickoff return by DeAndre Carter gave the offense premium field position at the Titans 34.

Not to score an offensive touchdown has to be bothersome for coordinator Shane Waldron.

Protection wasn’t always great, and it appeared the Bears had issues on the interior against standout defensive tackle Jeffery Simmons and rookie T’Vondre Sweat, who was a rock in the middle at 362 pounds. Williams tried to escape by going backward, and he’ll learn quickly that retreating in the NFL is usually a bad idea.

“I would say offensive play — obviously, we didn’t perform how we wanted to,” Williams said. “We want to be the most efficient team out there, myself included. Didn’t perform the way I wanted to. I missed a few passes that I normally don’t miss. All these other things. I think there’s small things that always lead up to big things, make those moments and things like that a lot bigger, that make games a lot closer.

“Just the small things, whether it’s a pass play that I had to D’Andre Swift in the flat that I missed a little bit in front of him, whether it’s missing (DJ Moore) a little bit on the end route on the backside, a little bit in front of him. All these small moments that happen throughout the game that felt like I was in the right place, the right time, and I would say I was seeing it well. Just missed and misfired and (not) placing the ball where I wanted to.”

Williams fired too long for Carter, who was open behind the Tennessee secondary on third-and-4 from midfield in the second quarter. The second-reaction plays that led to big profits in the preseason, sometimes against the opponent’s reserves, didn’t materialize. The Titans found a way to have someone in his face or in close pursuit when he was outside the pocket, and while he took only two sacks, the Bears didn’t get those chunk plays needed to ignite a drive.

“We made it hard for him with different things we do defensively,” Titans safety Quandre Diggs said. “He’s learning. I think today was a good learning experience for him. No matter if people say he played bad, he was able to get a win today. We had the momentum. If we would have gotten the ball (from Williams), that would have really flipped it.”

Combined with a rushing attack that struggled to get going — the only carry by a running back that went for more than 6 yards was Swift’s 20-yard gain with less than four minutes to play when he hurdled Diggs — the Bears were stuck in the mud for four quarters.

“We got stalled,” center Coleman Shelton said. “We never really got in a rhythm as an offense, and you can see we finally started getting in it toward the end of the game there on that drive (when the Bears went from their 11 to the Tennessee 43 before punting with 2:33 to play). So it was really trying to find a rhythm, find our groove and get going.

“(Williams) was great at staying positive and keeping us going and realizing we were in the game the whole time.”

The off-schedule plays will come. Williams’ skill will be on display and he’ll have to iron out some of the small timing issues that seemed to be just off in the passing game. Waldron needs to find a rhythm.

“It’s just not being on the same page passing-wise,” Williams said. “And run game, we’ll be better. Obviously it’s great to get this first win. And we’re all excited, went into the locker room, celebrated. I sat down, enjoyed the moment.

“And just watching the other guys, I understand that I need to be better. I will be better. And then just taking in the first one. It’s unbelievable.”

2. Defensive end Darrell Taylor got off to a big start with the Bears.

“Hard Knocks” captured GM Ryan Poles, after negotiations broke down with the New England Patriots early in training camp to trade for Matthew Judon, telling director of football administration Matthew Feinstein that if Judon “has 20 sacks or something, yeah, it’ll make me sick.”

Imagine how Poles will feel if Taylor, whom the Bears acquired before roster cuts for a sixth-round draft pick, has 12 sacks.

Taylor had two sacks and a career-high eight tackles Sunday, with his strip-sack of Will Levis in the fourth quarter setting up a 48-yard field goal by Cairo Santos.



It could be the Bears found just the right player with the right skill set to line up opposite Montez Sweat. Scouts say Taylor has speed and a good get-off and can be proficient hunting the quarterback, which is supported by his 21½ sacks for the Seattle Seahawks over the previous three seasons.

He isn’t considered great against the run, but the Bears traded for him to boost their pass rush and if Sunday’s debut is a sign of things to come, they’re going to use him primarily on passing downs.

The Bears had chipped into the Tennessee lead but the Titans were on their own 48-yard line on third-and-6 when Matt Eberflus blitzed with middle linebacker Tremaine Edmunds coming up the middle. Running back Tyjae Spears chopped down Edmunds, but Levis wasn’t able to climb the pocket and floated out to his right.

Taylor used a jab step on Titans left tackle and first-round draft pick JC Latham and quickly swiped his hands. Then it was a foot race — what Taylor does best.

“I just came off the edge and saw the ball was within my reach,” said Taylor, who knocked the ball out and weak-side linebacker T.J. Edwards recovered.

What does that look like, especially in the fourth quarter of a one-score game?

“Your eyes get big,” Taylor said as his eyes bulged out of his head. “It’s like seeing a million dollars, really. So my eyes got big and I knew it was an opportunity for us to cause a turnover and give the ball back to our offense.”

Taylor is in a contract year and earning $3.136 million this season. He got the nod over rookie fifth-round pick Austin Booker and Daniel Hardy, who both played well in the preseason. The Bears opted to go with experience more often, and Booker had a tough time on the Tennessee drive that finished with a 26-yard touchdown run by Tony Pollard. The Titans were running right at Booker.

Said Taylor: “It definitely feels great — it’s my debut and I show my teammates what I am all about and show them what I am willing to do for this team.”

When he met with reporters after the trade last month, Taylor’s head still was spinning in his new surroundings. He was processing being part of a business transaction. The team that drafted him had flipped him for a late Day 3 draft pick as the Seahawks deemed he wasn’t a great fit for what they’re transitioning to on defense.

“It definitely hurts,” he said. “But that’s what the league is about. Trades happen every year. That’s why I am here and I’m here to make an impact on this defense and on this team. That is my only goal.”

I pointed out the metaphor Taylor used when he described seeing Levis holding the ball as he turned the corner could apply to him at the end of the season as well. There’s a bag of money — with way more than a million dollars in it — if he has a big season.

“It’s definitely sitting there,” Taylor said. “For sure.”

And if Taylor can pursue that bag after the season — from the Bears or any other team — Poles will feel pretty damn good. Judon, by the way, had five tackles and a half-sack in his debut for the Atlanta Falcons, an 18-10 home loss to the Pittsburgh Steelers.

3. Some shrewd adjustments on defense and strong play by the secondary swung this game for the Bears.

Bears cornerback Tyrique Stevenson scores on an interception return against the Titans in the fourth quarter on Sept. 8, 2024, at Soldier Field. (Brian Cassella/Chicago Tribune) Bears cornerback Tyrique Stevenson scores on an interception return against the Titans in the fourth quarter on Sept. 8, 2024, at Soldier Field. (Brian Cassella/Chicago Tribune) That and the scoop-and-score on the team’s first blocked punt in 12 years.

The Titans controlled the game in the first half because they found success on the ground. Tony Pollard had nine carries for 64 yards at halftime and Tennessee had 16 rushes for 115 yards in the first half against the NFL’s No. 1 run defense in 2023.

It looked like Matt Eberflus switched things up in the second half and went to a Wide 9 look with his defensive ends. The Titans were attacking them on the edges in the first half — that’s where Pollard’s long touchdown run came — and countering, and the Bears couldn’t contain the attack.

So the Bears widened out their ends with a goal of funneling the action back inside, and Tennessee kept trying to go there with no success. The Titans had only 25 rushing yards in the second half on 11 carries. By widening out up front, the Bears created more one-on-one blocks and were able to win more of them.

The idea of halftime adjustments is often overblown. Adjustments happen all the time on the sideline. There is a small army of coaches in a box, a sideline full of tablets with all the plays to review and coaches to make shifts or subtle changes on the go. The 12 minutes teams have at halftime is a chance for a quick breather, a bathroom break, some fluids and a few coaching points. That’s it.

But from the look of things, the Bears adjusted Sunday and the Titans didn’t have a counter.

Eberflus was also aggressive in going after Will Levis. It sure looked like the Bears were comfortable putting their cornerbacks in man-to-man situations on more than a few occasions. Levis overshot Calvin Ridley when Ridley had Tyrique Stevenson beat down the sideline.

But the Bears held up, the rush got to Levis and the cornerbacks made huge plays at big moments, none bigger than Stevenson’s 43-yard interception return for a go-ahead touchdown with 7:35 remaining.

Levis tried to flip the ball away for an incomplete pass as DeMarcus Walker was wrapping him up, one of four quarterback hits for Walker. It was the kind of terrible play that will be on Levis’ “highlight” reel for months if he continues to struggle.

“I was thinking sack, let’s get off the field, good (stuff) there for the defense,” Stevenson said. “And then, in a split-second, he let it go. And then, I ain’t going to lie to you, next thing I know I am in the end zone doing my dance. Everything was just a blur.”

Levis didn’t factor in the game situation. The Titans were ahead 17-16 and it was third down on their 45-yard line. Take the sack and even with a modest punt, the Bears are starting inside their 30.

“The way our defense was playing, it’s killer,” Titans coach Brian Callahan said. “It’s killer.”

This underlies my point about Williams not making the kind of catastrophic mistakes young quarterbacks commit that simply doom their teams.

“That thing is going to be on blooper reels for the rest of his career,” Stevenson said. “He lost the game based off him making a decision he didn’t have to make. Eat it, punt it and make our offense come back. Instead, he throws it to me.”

Jaylon Johnson added a pick to seal the game when Levis, again under pressure (the Bears totaled 10 QB hits), lofted a pass off his back foot. As out of sorts as the Bears were on offense, they found the necessary adjustments to sync on defense and that propelled the rally.

4. Daniel Hardy’s 40 special teams snaps in the preseason were probably just as important as his 3½ sacks in finding a spot on the 53-man roster.

Bears safety Jonathan Owens returned the ball for a touchdown. (Chris Sweda/Chicago Tribune) If you followed the clues the coaching staff left — Matt Eberflus and others talked about what great shape Hardy was in and how well he was moving — it was clear the best chance Hardy had to earn a job was proving he could help on special teams. When the Bears decided to go heavy at defensive end — they kept six on the 53-man roster (one or two more than most teams) — Hardy had a spot.

He took a step toward securing that position with a blocked punt midway through the third quarter that safety Jonathan Owens scooped up and returned 21 yards for a touchdown. The Bears looked well on their way to defeat at the time. Little was going right and if the Titans played mostly mistake-free football (they did not), it would be a disappointing opener for the home crowd.



Hardy overpowered linebacker Jake Gibbens and was on top of punter Ryan Stonehouse for the Bears’ first blocked punt since a 2012 meeting with the Titans. It was a pressure that special teams coordinator Richard Hightower put on. His unit, with the exception of the massive gaffe by Velus Jones Jr., played very well and played hard.

It appeared the Bears looped some players to the left on the block and got the guard to lean inside. Hardy had a clear path. Earlier in the game, the Bears tried confusing the Titans by rolling a cornerback on a punt.

“It’s an amazing feeling to be able to accomplish something like that with your teammates, and I wanted to find Coach Hightower and let him know,” Hardy said. “As a special teams coordinator, you’ve got to put a lot of pressure on guys to be able to call something like that. You can run into the punter and the offense has a fresh set of downs. I wanted to let him know I had his back.

“It is something we drew up. Something we had been working on all training camp. And you know, just a huge thank you to Coach Hightower for trusting us to get that done. We were feeling the momentum a little bit but we needed a spark on teams, we needed to help fuel the offense, defense, everything. Fantastic call trusting us to get the job done.”

Hardy, who had 128 snaps on special teams for the Los Angeles Rams in 2022, knows that’s where he’ll have to make his biggest impact right now.

“Making the 53 is more than just playing on offense and defense,” he said. “They gave me a job and I am doing the best I can with it. Just being grateful to be here. Not taking anything for granted. I am looking for ways to impact the game in a positive way.”

5. With a chuckle and an affirming nod, Mark Sanchez looked at his Week 1 assignment when Fox unveiled its schedule for announcers to open the season.

USC quarterback Mark Sanchez puts on a Jets cap on April 25, 2009, after he got the word that he was drafted. (Ken Hively/Los Angeles Times) USC quarterback Mark Sanchez puts on a Jets cap on April 25, 2009, after he got the word that he was drafted. (Ken Hively/Los Angeles Times) There isn’t another broadcaster at the network — maybe anywhere — who better understands the situation, pressure and expectations that will be and already have been thrust upon Caleb Williams.

Once upon a time, Sanchez was the fifth pick in the 2009 draft by the New York Jets, the heir apparent to franchise icon Joe Namath. Sanchez, who was a backup for the Bears in 2017, doesn’t just have an idea of what Williams will encounter for another quarterback-starved franchise. He has walked a very similar path.

Sanchez, a tanned and toned California boy, was the next coming of Broadway Joe at the beginning of his career, and the hype (and hope) only grew as the Jets reached the AFC championship game in his first two seasons.

So if anyone can describe what Williams will experience in the beginning of his NFL journey, it’s Sanchez, who also starred at USC and got to know Williams during the last two seasons in Los Angeles.

“It’s so funny,” Sanchez said Wednesday. “How much this plays into this broadcast. Not about me but more about the situation. I’ve got to go in there and replace Joe Namath and he’s got to go in there and be Sid Luckman and Jim McMahon. That’s a lot.

“How many times have these fans had a preseason Hall of Famer in the last 20, 30 years? ‘Hey, this is the guy! This is the guy!’ Somehow they’re not there. It’s not always the player’s fault. Sometimes it’s the situation. Sometimes it’s the circumstances, but that’s stuff that you can’t really focus on but there is just a lot of noise to it.

“And you win the first game out of the gate and it’s, ‘Yep, we got our guy.’ You lose the first game out of the gate and it’s, ‘Oh, man, here we go again.’ Navigating those seas is difficult for a young guy. That market is every bit as tough as New York because there is only one team. At least in New York if you’re playing well and the Giants stink or the other way around, they can kind of deflect some of the fire coming your way. It’s a white-hot spotlight and that’s not easy.”

It’s so hot, the Bears’ last two first-round picks at the position before Williams — Justin Fields and Mitch Trubisky — each explained at different times why they had shelved their social media accounts when the noise and frustration of the fan base escalated. It’s a pressure cooker trying to be the answer for an organization that, through no fault of the current quarterback, has bungled the position time and time again, regime after regime.

That’s precisely one of the pitfalls ahead of Williams, the kind of cavernous drop that can swallow a quarterback, all of his promise, all of his talent, and leave everyone to extend their arms and ask the same question: “Where did it go wrong?”

“The default answer is try and block it out and focus on what’s important and win on Wednesday so you can win on Sunday, all those cliches that everybody is going to tell him,” Sanchez said. “But it is nearly impossible in today’s world to just completely tune that out. It’s impossible with the phones and all the social media stuff, and he’s a guy that is really the first superstar NIL player, the first of his kind. Not like LeBron James, who went high school to pro, but that kind of projection, you know what I mean?

“And I don’t know what you say to him other than, ‘Yeah, you try to block it out but you’re inevitably going to see some of it and people are going to bring it up when you’re signing autographs, it’s going to come up in press conferences.’ They’re already telling him nobody has thrown for 4,000 yards or 30 touchdowns. This guy has got to go win games. Don’t worry about all of that. All of that stuff is going to take care of itself and a lot of that stuff is out of your hands.”

Getting to know Williams as he has — Sanchez said he has tried to act as a sounding board and isn’t one to reach out unless he’s asked — the 37-year-old wonders if the Bears rookie is uniquely wired to thrive under the spotlight instead of melting. Sure, it’s a dangerous line to walk, but the only chance for success is for Williams to be true to himself — he is who he is.

Sanchez/Chicago Tribune) “It’s going to be interesting to watch and he’s one of those guys that, in some ways, feeds off all of that stuff, which is part of his personality,” Sanchez said. “So telling him to lock it up and totally change his approach, I don’t think that is the right advice either because he’s more active on the social media platform and he has other interests outside of football. But you lose three games in a row and everyone is going to say, ‘Oh, man, that fashion show in Milan doesn’t sound so good.’ That’s the way it goes.

“The GQ cover in New York (in September 2011), people gave me heat for that. ‘He spent all that time at the photo shoot.’ The photo shoot was five hours. But you throw an interception and it’s, ‘Well, you could have been studying your playbook.’ Is that totally fair? No. But the thing Caleb signed up for isn’t totally fair. It’s so funny he could be an absolute superstar there. There is buzz around him. They’re comparing him to Michael Jordan and I’m like, ‘Holy smokes!’ That’s a lot. How are you going to handle that?

“Some of the advice I would give is bring the guys with you. Bring them to the movie premieres and Broadway shows and fashion shows and all of the stuff that you can do individually unless it’s something you are passionate about that is kind of your one thing. But bring them with you. When I got invited to Broadway shows, I wouldn’t go unless I had 10 tickets. I’d decline. Because when you go as a group it turns into a team bonding session instead of just, ‘Hey, this guy is out and about. He doesn’t focus. He’s got too many interests. He’s got too many irons in the fire.’ That turns down the heat a little and it gives him time to learn his guys.”

Sanchez pointed out the Hollywood narrative that Williams is cast in, at least in some spaces, and laughs.

“He’s not even from Southern California,” Sanchez said. “He was there two seasons. I was. I was just this surfer dude, GQ, pretty boy from California, and that is a tough narrative in a tough East Coast city, New York, where you hear, ‘You ain’t ready for this.’

“Then, when people got to meet me, got to know me, all right, maybe he is OK. You can’t fight that initial stigma. This guy (Williams) is from D.C.”

Nor can Williams battle the storyline that he’s here to replace Luckman, McMahon, whoever and lead the Bears in ways they’ve only imagined, at least in the Super Bowl era. Maybe Sanchez is right. Maybe his understanding of the obstacles ahead of Williams and the way he’s wired is accurate. Just maybe Williams is ready for this.

Sunday’s debut wasn’t pretty, but Williams was measured afterward. He knows he has to be better.

6. The Bears already have given up on Velus Jones Jr. as a punt returner.

(Brian Cassella/Chicago Tribune) Last month they effectively gave up on him as a wide receiver in a crowded position room and switched him to running back. And if he cannot return kickoffs for them, well, he’s running out of ways to make himself useful.

Jones found a solid role as a kickoff returner last season. He averaged 27.4 yards per return on 16 attempts, and there was a feeling the NFL rule changes on kickoffs would make him a potentially bigger weapon.

So when Jones muffed the first kickoff return of the season — then inadvertently kicked the ball forward from the 3-yard line to the 23, where Tennessee’s Julius Chestnut recovered — it was “here we go again.”

Jones’ mishaps on punt returns are well-documented. He had two killer muffs as a rookie in 2022 that directly led to losses to the New York Giants and Washington Commanders. He had a third fumble that season on offense against the Philadelphia Eagles and a dropped touchdown pass.

The Bears yanked him from punt returns and he hasn’t been seen there again. They pulled him from kickoff returns after Sunday’s mishap, pairing DeAndre Carter, the new punt returner, with running back Khalil Herbert.

“I just took my eyes off the ball,” Jones said. “I’ve caught that ball like a million times. So it’s like sometimes you can get lackadaisical. Then I didn’t realize when I looked for it, I actually kicked it. So that was just an unfortunate thing. Never happened to me before. It’s the small details. Just got to go back to square one, just looking everything in.”

Jones’ momentum was going forward when the mistake happened. It was setting up to be perhaps the start of a big return because he would have been at full speed in an instant. And then the ball hit off his chest and — pfft! — was gone.

“Facts, facts, facts,” Jones said. “It really sucks, but we got the win at the end of the day and I appreciate my teammates for having my back and, you know, that was where I am just trying to focus on the next play.”

He still got some spot play on offense — he had two carries for 11 yards and caught one pass for 8 yards — but the old saying is “the more you can do,” and you have to start wondering what the Bears have in mind for Jones if there’s actually less he can do.

“It’s no concern,” he said when I asked about potentially being benched on kickoff returns. “Obviously I am a competitor. I know what I bring to the table, and like I said, that was unfortunate. I know the staff believes in me. They’ve seen me return how many kicks? Really, ain’t much to say about that. Definitely that can’t happen.”

Matt Eberflus supported Jones after the game and that’s not surprising. Coaches rarely predict a player’s demise in a postgame setting. But as giddy as the Bears were to keep Jones on the 53-man roster in the final episode of “Hard Knocks,” their faith just had a big hole poked in the middle of it.

“I was proud of Velus,” Eberflus said. “I really was. I know that wasn’t what he wanted, obviously. He did a nice job coming in at the halfback position, did a nice job getting north. I thought he did well. We’ll evaluate in terms of kick return as we go.”

7. DeeJay Dallas is the answer to the trivia question that will come up years from now.

Barnes) The Cardinals’ DeeJay Dallas returns a kickoff for a touchdown against the Bills during the second half on Sept. 8, 2024, in Orchard Park, N.Y. (AP Photo/Jeffrey T. Barnes) Who was the first player to return a kickoff for a touchdown after the NFL made massive changes to the special teams play in 2024?

Dallas of the Arizona Cardinals hit the Buffalo Bills for a 96-yard touchdown Sunday, the only score on a kickoff through the first 15 games of the season. DeAndre Carter of the Bears had the second-longest return — 67 yards. There was one other return of more than 50 yards heading into the Monday night game between the New York Jets and San Francisco 49ers.

What does the small sample size show us so far? There were returns on 34.6% of kickoffs, so the number has increased but maybe not to the level the league imagined. The competition committee’s newfangled approach to the kickoff was put in place to encourage more returns. The league has been crusading for ways to make the play safer for years with a goal of reducing head injuries, but an all-time low 21.8% of kickoffs were returned last season.

Some teams didn’t fear the possibility of a big return. The Bears covered five kickoffs and had only one touchback for Cairo Santos. The New Orleans Saints covered nine returns in a blowout victory over the Carolina Panthers with only one touchback. The Panthers had a long return of 31 yards and averaged 25.8 yards on their nine attempts.

The Kansas City Chiefs, New England Patriots, Atlanta Falcons, Indianapolis Colts, New York Giants and Los Angeles Rams booted nothing but touchbacks.

The tally going into Monday night: 156 kickoffs, 54 returns, 100 touchbacks and two kicks out of bounds.

Fifteen games is a small sample size — and the ball will fly in outdoor stadiums while the weather is warm — but the results were a dramatic departure from what we saw in the preseason, which is not surprising. There were returns on 70.5% of kickoffs in the preseason as teams wanted to see how the new rules worked and test various strategies.

Then real games began Thursday in Kansas City, and the kickoff was back to being a play with rare action. There were 11 kickoffs between the Chiefs and Baltimore Ravens and nine touchbacks spotted at the 30-yard line. 6. Evaluating offensive line play is a difficult task.

Bears offensive tackle Riley Reiff (71) and teammates wait for the next play in the first quarter against the Vikings at Soldier Field on Jan. 8, 2023.

Bears offensive tackle Riley Reiff (71) and teammates wait for the next play in the first quarter against the Vikings at Soldier Field on Jan. 8, 2023. (John J. Kim / Chicago Tribune)

Grading players is guesswork when you don’t know the specific protection assignment on any given play. On occasion it’s obvious to anyone viewing where the blame lies. But more often than not, there is some gray involved. A full understanding requires knowing what the quarterback’s progression was and how the receivers executed their routes.

You don’t need various rankings or grades to know the Bears were very poor in protecting Justin Fields and the other quarterbacks. What struck me back in July was when Ross Tucker, who has a network of podcasts and does work for CBS Sports, Audacy and Westwood One, stacked up the league’s 32 offensive lines and had the Bears last.

“The Bears have the potential to not only have the worst offensive line in the NFL this year but maybe even the worst offensive line we’ve seen in the NFL in quite some time if their young tackles don’t come through,” Tucker wrote.

[ [Don’t miss] How do the 2022 Chicago Bears compare with the worst teams in franchise history? A look at some of the ‘darkest and dreariest’ years. ]

During the first week of training camp, I reached out to Tucker, who is always entertaining and informative. If you haven’t seen his #tuckspreads on social media, it’s amusing as he travels to pro and college stadiums. He’s a former offensive lineman, so he has a pretty good idea of what he’s looking at in the trenches — and he’s not a hot-take artist. Tucker gives you a measured take and works tirelessly to follow the entire league.

“If (Fields) doesn’t have a lot of turnovers and he is getting the ball out quick, I feel like that will be considered a success no matter what the team’s record is or the stats because they’re just not very good around him,” Tucker told me after his rankings came out.

“You can make a strong argument that they have the worst receiving corps and the worst offensive line in the NFL. It’s almost ... unconscionable that a first-round quarterback would be going into his second year and you could say that is what he is being surrounded by.”

Turnovers were an issue for the Bears. Fields’ interception rate of 3.5% was 32nd out of 33 quarterbacks with a minimum of 200 attempts. Only the Cowboys’ Dak Prescott, who objectively had a very rough second half of the season, had a higher rate at 3.9%.

Fields led the NFL with 16 fumbles and remarkably lost only two. He has 28 fumbles in 27 career games with seven lost, so ball security no doubt will be an offseason focus.

The upshot? The Bears have a ton of work to do in improving the offensive line and adding targets for the quarterback. It’s probably a good idea to wait and see what predictions Tucker has in midsummer. Maybe he will have the Super Bowl LVIII winner — or at least a better review of the Bears offensive line.

8. One way to take some hits off Justin Fields next season would be to start throwing to the running backs more.

Bears running back David Montgomery high-fives fans before heading to the locker room after a 29-13 loss to the Vikings at Soldier Field on Jan. 8, 2023.

Bears running back David Montgomery high-fives fans before heading to the locker room after a 29-13 loss to the Vikings at Soldier Field on Jan. 8, 2023. (John J. Kim / Chicago Tribune)

That’s something the offense didn’t do enough of as David Montgomery finished with a team-high 34 receptions out of the backfield. Montgomery had six games with two targets or fewer, excluding the Week 3 win over the Texans in which he exited with a knee injury. That’s not enough. Khalil Herbert finished with nine catches, and the team’s running backs had a total of 46.

The Bears, who entered Week 18 ranked 21st in the NFL in target percentage for running backs at 17.5%, were pretty efficient when they did throw to backs. They had only 61 targets for running backs, so a 73.8% completion percentage is a positive.

Montgomery isn’t a dynamic receiver. No one will mistake him for Austin Ekeler or Christian McCaffrey but he’s solid. He caught 42 passes a season ago, when he missed two games, and had 54 receptions in 2020. Pass catching is a deficiency for Herbert, but he still caught nine of 12 targets for 57 yards. Since 2006, the only season the Bears’ leading receiver out of the backfield had fewer than 34 catches was 2016 (Jordan Howard with 29).

One explanation for the backs not catching more passes is the Bears probably leaned on max protection a little more than they would have preferred, keeping a back in to support pass blocking. Another is that Fields didn’t get to his check-down targets quickly enough, resulting in hits in the pocket or on a scramble. If the primary read is covered, get it to the back and check down for 6 yards, then move to the next play. That saves the quarterback from having to run and being exposed to defenders.

Offensive coordinator Luke Getsy was part of an offense in Green Bay that featured running back Aaron Jones as a pass catcher at times. Who knows what shape the backfield takes in 2023 with Montgomery headed to free agency. But the Bears can take some hits off Fields by using the backs more in the passing game.
